The Federal Reserve held its benchmark rate steady at its March meeting, as widely expected. For buyers in the Twin Cities market, here's what that actually means.

## The Fed rate vs. mortgage rates

First, a clarification that trips up a lot of buyers: the Fed doesn't set mortgage rates. The federal funds rate influences the broader interest rate environment, but 30-year mortgage rates are primarily driven by the 10-year Treasury yield and secondary mortgage market conditions.

The practical implication: even when the Fed holds steady or cuts, mortgage rates don't necessarily follow immediately or proportionally. The relationship is real but indirect.

## Where rates stand

30-year fixed rates are currently in the mid-to-upper 6% range for well-qualified buyers. That's meaningfully higher than the 3% environment of 2021, but historically not unusual. Rates in the 6-7% range were the norm for most of the 1990s and 2000s.

The pain buyers feel isn't really about the rate in isolation — it's about the combination of elevated rates and elevated prices. Both went up simultaneously, which compressed affordability significantly from the 2020-2021 baseline.

## What it means for your purchase power

At 6.75% on a 30-year fixed, every $100,000 of loan amount costs approximately $648/month in principal and interest. Put another way:

- $400,000 loan → ~$2,594/month P&I
- $500,000 loan → ~$3,243/month P&I  
- $600,000 loan → ~$3,891/month P&I

These numbers are why down payment strategy matters more than it used to. A larger down payment reduces the loan amount and therefore the monthly payment — more so than waiting for rates to drop by a quarter point.

## Should you wait for rates to drop?

This is the question I get most often. My honest answer: probably not, for most buyers.

Rate forecasting is genuinely difficult. Economists have been predicting meaningful rate cuts for over a year; they haven't materialized at the expected pace or magnitude. Every month you wait is a month of rent paid with no equity accumulation, and in a market like Crocus Hill or Mac-Groveland, a month of appreciation can easily offset a modest rate improvement.

The buyers who are doing well right now underwrote their purchase at current rates, found a payment they can genuinely afford, and focused on finding the right house — not the right rate environment.

If rates drop meaningfully after you buy, you refinance. That optionality exists. The house you passed on does not.

## The bottom line

The Fed holding steady is neither good nor bad news for buyers in the short term. It means the rate environment is likely to remain roughly where it is for the foreseeable future. Plan accordingly, buy what you can afford at today's rates, and let time do its work.
